## [Gravitational lens shows a galaxy just 800 million years post-Big Bang](https://arstechnica.com/science/2026/05/gravitational-lens-shows-a-galaxy-just-800-million-years-post-big-bang/) ⭐️ 6.0/10

Astronomers employed gravitational lensing to observe a galaxy located just 800 million years after the Big Bang, successfully detecting chemical elements that were produced by the universe's first generation of supernovae. This observation provides direct evidence of how the first supernovae enriched the early universe with heavy elements, offering crucial insights into cosmic chemical evolution during the first billion years after the Big Bang. The gravitational lensing effect amplified the distant galaxy's light, making it detectable despite its extreme distance. The detected elements confirm that early stellar nucleosynthesis had already begun producing heavier elements within the first few hundred million years of cosmic history.

rss · Ars Technica · May 13, 15:53

**Background**: Gravitational lensing occurs when a massive object (such as a galaxy cluster) bends and magnifies light from a more distant object behind it, acting like a cosmic magnifying glass. This phenomenon, predicted by Einstein's General Relativity, allows astronomers to observe objects too faint or distant for conventional telescopes. The first supernovae were massive stars that ended their lives in spectacular explosions, dispersing elements like carbon, oxygen, and iron into the interstellar medium, paving the way for subsequent generations of stars and planets.
