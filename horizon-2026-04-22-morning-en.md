## [How GPS Works: Atomic Clocks and Relativity Explained](https://perthirtysix.com/how-the-heck-does-gps-work) ⭐️ 7.0/10

An explainer article was published detailing how GPS works, covering atomic clock synchronization, relativistic time dilation effects requiring a ~38 microseconds/day correction, and how ground stations use Very-long-baseline interferometry to track satellite positions. GPS is ubiquitous yet often taken for granted — understanding how it works reveals the remarkable precision engineering and relativistic physics required to make navigation accurate. This knowledge bridges fundamental physics with everyday technology that billions of people rely on. GPS satellites carry atomic clocks accurate to 1 nanosecond, and the system must account for both special AND general relativistic effects — satellites experience different gravitational fields and time dilation due to orbital velocity. The combined relativistic correction factor is approximately 38 microseconds per day, small enough to ignore in everyday life but catastrophic for GPS accuracy if unaccounted for.

hackernews · alfanick · Apr 22, 09:22

**Background**: GPS (Global Positioning System) consists of approximately 31 active satellites orbiting at about 20,200 km altitude, continuously broadcasting their position and time from atomic clocks. Ground receivers calculate distance by measuring signal travel time from multiple satellites, then use trilateration to determine location. Around the world, fundamental ground stations use Very-long-baseline interferometry — measuring time differences of quasar radio signals across radio telescopes — to precisely track their own positions relative to each other.

## [Why XOR Trumps SUB for Register Zeroing](https://devblogs.microsoft.com/oldnewthing/20260421-00/?p=112247) ⭐️ 7.0/10

An exploration of why XOR is the preferred assembly idiom for zeroing registers instead of SUB, with insights into CPU architecture, pipelining, and historical context from different processor generations. This optimization choice impacts code size, execution performance, and pipeline efficiency. Understanding the rationale helps developers write better assembly and appreciate low-level CPU design decisions. The SUB instruction requires carry propagation across all bits, creating sequential dependencies. XOR operates bitwise with independent calculations, enabling parallel execution. However, Itanium doesn't support this trick due to NaT bit preservation.

hackernews · ingve · Apr 22, 06:38

**Background**: Register zeroing is a common operation in assembly programming. While SUB reg, 0 would technically yield zero, XOR reg, reg is preferred on x86 due to its compact 2-byte encoding and ability to break dependency chains. RISC architectures like MIPS and ARM often have dedicated zero registers for this purpose, while x86 relies on idiomatic instructions.

hackernews · sohkamyung · Apr 22, 09:52
