## [Turkish Grand Prix Returns to F1 Calendar with Five-Year Deal](https://www.racefans.net/2026/04/24/turkish-grand-prix-to-return-to-f1-calendar-in-five-year-deal/) ⭐️ 3.0/10

Formula 1 is expected to confirm a five-year deal to bring the Turkish Grand Prix back to the calendar starting in 2027 at Istanbul Park. The race will mark the return of a venue that has previously hosted F1 between 2005-2011 and again briefly in 2020-2021. The return of the Turkish Grand Prix restores access to a unique circuit known for its technical 5.338-kilometer layout featuring dramatic elevation changes. For F1, it strengthens presence in a strategic geographic market bridging Europe and Asia, while giving fans a track that has historically delivered exciting races. The deal is reportedly structured as a five-year commitment through at least 2031. Istanbul Park features a technical layout with 19 corners and is one of the few anti-clockwise circuits on the F1 calendar. The venue gained fame for producing dramatic races, including a memorable 2020 edition won by Lewis Hamilton on a shortened, wet track.

**Background**: Istanbul Park was designed by architect Herman Tilke and opened in 2005. The circuit quickly became a fan favorite during its initial F1 tenure, known for its challenging turns and spectator-friendly layout with views of nearly the entire track from the main grandstand. Negotiations for the race's return reportedly took several years amid varying political and economic conditions in Turkey.

## [F1 2026 Rule Changes Will Make Qualifying Cars Deliberately Slower](https://www.the-race.com/formula-1/how-urgent-f1-2026-rule-changes-will-actually-work/) ⭐️ 3.0/10

New data reveals that F1's 2026 rule changes will intentionally slow down cars during qualifying sessions. The FIA is reducing the energy recovery system recharge limit from 8MJ to 7MJ per lap, while also changing the qualifying format to eliminate the slowest six cars instead of five in both Q1 and Q2. These rule modifications fundamentally change how teams approach qualifying strategy. By limiting available energy recovery, drivers cannot extract maximum performance on single flying laps, making racing more about consistency and skill rather than pure mechanical advantage. The recharge limit reduction directly impacts how much hybrid energy can be deployed for extra power during qualifying laps. Combined with the new flat floor replacing ground-effect tunnels, the 2026 cars will have fundamentally different aerodynamic characteristics compared to their predecessors.

**Background**: F1 qualifying uses a knockout format where drivers complete timed laps across three sessions. The energy recovery system (ERS) captures heat and braking energy to supplement the internal combustion engine, providing additional horsepower. Ground-effect aerodynamics, which use venturi tunnels to generate downforce with minimal drag, were reintroduced in 2022 to allow closer racing. For 2026, the FIA is replacing these tunnels with a flatter floor design to reduce the complexity of dirty air affecting following cars.
