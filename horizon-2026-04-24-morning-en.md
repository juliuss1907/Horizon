Hospitals worldwide are rapidly deploying AI tools for clinical note-taking, patient record analysis, and medical image interpretation. However, a critical gap exists: there is limited rigorous evidence demonstrating that these AI systems actually improve patient outcomes. This disconnect between widespread AI deployment and evidence of benefit raises serious concerns about patient safety, healthcare costs, and regulatory oversight. Patients and healthcare systems may be investing in tools without proven clinical value, potentially diverting resources from interventions with established efficacy. AI applications currently deployed in hospitals include automated clinical documentation, predictive algorithms that flag patients requiring specific treatments, and computer vision systems for interpreting X-rays and medical examinations. Despite this rapid adoption, few randomized controlled trials or rigorous outcome studies have been conducted to validate these tools' effectiveness in real clinical settings.

rss · MIT Tech Review · Apr 24, 09:00

**Background**: The healthcare industry has experienced a dramatic surge in AI adoption, driven by promises of improved efficiency, reduced physician burnout, and enhanced diagnostic accuracy. Unlike traditional medical devices that require extensive clinical trials and regulatory approval, AI healthcare tools have entered clinical practice with minimal evidence requirements. This creates a concerning precedent where potentially transformative technology is being widely deployed before its benefits are adequately demonstrated through rigorous scientific validation.

## [Pentagon Spectrum Reform: Commercial-First Model Proposed](https://warontherocks.com/cogs-of-war/airwaves-of-power-why-the-pentagon-should-shift-to-a-commercial-first-spectrum-model/) ⭐️ 6.0/10

A policy analysis from War on the Rocks argues that the Pentagon should adopt a commercial-first spectrum model, where the military preserves exclusive spectrum access only where operationally necessary while otherwise becoming the primary adopter of secure commercial wireless capabilities. This shift matters because the Department of Defense currently occupies prime mid-band frequencies for relatively low-throughput national security uses, while those same bands could generate much larger commercial, allied, and strategic returns if reallocated. The proposed model would allow the Pentagon to shape standards and harden security for commercial technologies while freeing up spectrum for broader use. Mid-band spectrum (3.3-4.2 GHz) is particularly valuable for 5G applications due to its balance of coverage and data transfer rates.

rss · War on the Rocks · Apr 24, 08:00

**Background**: Radio frequency spectrum is a limited national resource managed by government agencies. In the U.S., the FCC oversees commercial spectrum allocation while the Department of Defense controls significant portions for military operations including radars, satellite communications, navigation, and electronic warfare. Mid-band spectrum has become particularly valuable for 5G networks as it offers a balance between coverage area and throughput compared to low-band and high-band (mmWave) alternatives.
