## [EFF to 4th Circuit: Electronic Device Searches at the Border Require a Warrant](https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/05/eff-fourth-circuit-electronic-device-searches-border-require-warrant) ⭐️ 8.0/10

The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) presented oral arguments before the Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals, arguing that warrantless searches of electronic devices at U.S. borders violate the Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches. The case stems from a defendant convicted of possessing child sexual abuse material (CSAM) whose electronic devices were searched without a warrant upon returning from South America. The ruling could establish binding precedent in the Fourth Circuit—covering Maryland, North Carolina, South Carolina, Virginia, and West Virginia—requiring warrants for electronic device searches at the border. Given that 80% of Americans live within 100 miles of any U.S. border under current government doctrine, this case could reshape Fourth Amendment protections for a vast majority of the population. The Fourth Circuit is evaluating whether the border search exception—which permits government agents to conduct searches at international borders without a warrant or probable cause—should apply to comprehensive forensic searches of electronic devices containing vast amounts of personal data. The trial court's ruling on the suppression motion is available in the court records, with commenters noting the government's justification relied on vague assertions that South America presents higher CSAM and sex trafficking risks.

hackernews · hn_acker · May 12, 21:48

**Background**: The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF), founded in 1990, is a non-profit digital rights organization that defends civil liberties in the digital world through impact litigation and amicus briefs. The Fourth Amendment to the U.S. Constitution protects against unreasonable searches, but the border search exception doctrine permits warrantless searches at international borders and their functional equivalents. Under current federal interpretation, the "border zone" extends 100 miles inland from any international border, including coastlines and the Great Lakes.
